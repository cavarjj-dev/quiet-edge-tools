What's Your Failure Response Pattern?

A self-audit for parents on how they show up when their athlete struggles

How you respond in the first moments after your athlete fails determines whether they develop resilience — or a fear of trying. This 10-question assessment helps you identify your default pattern so you can consciously move toward what actually builds them up.

Before you begin, bring a recent failure to mind — your athlete's last tough loss, missed goal, poor game, or disappointing result. Keep that specific moment in frame as you answer. Respond based on your honest, instinctive behavior — not how you wish you'd responded.

Your Take-Home Protocol

The next time your athlete has a setback, work through these four steps.

1
Acknowledge — Immediately Validate the emotion without minimizing or amplifying it. Name what you see without rushing past it.
"That really hurts. I know how much you wanted this."
2
Space — First 24 Hours No silver linings. No analysis. No comparisons to teammates. Give the emotion time to settle before you process it together.
(Silence and presence are enough. You don't have to fix anything yet.)
3
Process — After 24 Hours, When They're Ready Use the Result → Reason → Response framework. Let them do the analysis — you ask the questions.
"What do you think happened? What's in your control moving forward?"
4
Model It — Ongoing Let your athlete see you process your own failures. Say it out loud. This is the most powerful lesson you can teach.
"That didn't go the way I planned. Here's what I think happened. Here's what I'll try next."

Separating Failure from Identity

One of the most important things you can do when your athlete fails is help them separate the event from who they are. Your language in those first minutes shapes what they hear — even when you don't say either phrase directly.

"You had a bad game" — a fact about one performance.
"You're not good at this" — a story about who they are.

Your job is to keep these two things clearly separate in everything you say and do.
