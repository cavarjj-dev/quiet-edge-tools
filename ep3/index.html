Why This Works
Athletes who focus on controllable factors experience less anxiety and perform more consistently under pressure (Rotter, 1966). Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) research shows that psychological flexibility — the ability to redirect attention from uncontrollables to controllables — is one of the strongest predictors of sustained performance (Hayes et al., 2006). This worksheet trains that skill.
Instructions Time required: 15 minutes initially, then 2 minutes daily for 7 days
What you need: This worksheet, a pen
When to use: Start within 24 hours of watching Episode 3. Use the daily check before every practice or competition this week.

Check yourself: If you listed "winning" or "the score" inside your circle, move it out. You influence outcomes through your actions, but you don't control outcomes. Focus on the actions that give you the best chance.

Exercise 2: The Redirect Drill

When you catch yourself worrying about something outside your control, use this 3-step redirect:

Step What to Do Example
1. Catch it Notice you're focused on an uncontrollable "I'm thinking about whether the ref will be fair"
2. Name it Say: "That's outside my circle" "Ref calls are outside my circle"
3. Redirect Shift to one controllable action "I'm going to focus on my first-step quickness"

Reflection Questions

  1. How much of your mental energy this week was spent on things outside your control? If you could redirect even 20% of that energy to controllables, what would change?
  2. Think of an athlete you admire. What controllables do you think they focus on that other athletes ignore?
  3. The video connects to EP2 (self-talk): when you catch yourself worrying about an uncontrollable, what does your inner voice say? How could you use a self-talk replacement from EP2 to help redirect faster?
