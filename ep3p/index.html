Parent Responsibility Toolkit

Ownership Builds Confidence  ·  4 Sections  ·  ~15 minutes
The Core Distinction Teaching responsibility means guiding your athlete toward what they can control — effort, attitude, preparation, response to mistakes. Adding pressure means focusing their attention on outcomes they can't control. One empowers. One suffocates. This tool helps you close the gap between what you intend and what your athlete experiences.
1

Post-Game Question Audit — Replace Outcome Questions with Controllables

The questions you ask immediately after a game teach your athlete what matters. Outcome questions ("Did you win?" "Why didn't you score more?") create outcome anxiety. Controllables questions build responsibility and emotional resilience. Swap the default.

Outcome Questions — Retire These Controllables Questions — Use These Instead
"Did you win?" → "How was your effort today — 1 to 10?"
"Were you the best player out there?" → "How was your focus? Were you locked in?"
"Why didn't you score more?" → "Did you do what you've been working on?"
"Your teammates scored way more than you." → "How did you handle adversity when it came?"
"What does the coach think?" → "What's one thing you want to work on next time?"

Control Circle Teaching Guide — Teach It Explicitly With Your Athlete

Draw this circle with your athlete — not for them. The act of sorting what's inside vs. outside together builds the habit of asking "Can I control this?" before investing emotional energy. Add what's specific to your athlete's sport and situations below.

Inside the Circle

  • Effort
  • Attitude
  • Preparation
  • Focus
  • Response to mistakes
Add what applies to your athlete

Outside the Circle

  • Opponent strength
  • Referee calls
  • Weather / conditions
  • Playing time decisions
  • Others' opinions
What does your athlete stress about?
The Teaching Moment When your athlete is stressed, ask: "Is that inside or outside your circle?" — then redirect: "What can you actually do about it?" This is a two-question habit, not a lecture. Use it in the car, at dinner, at warmup. Repetition is what builds the skill.
3

Emotional Alignment Check — Do Your Reactions Match Your Values?

Kids read your emotional truth, not your words. If you say effort matters but you're visibly disappointed when they lose, they learn that results are what actually matter to you. Audit the gap honestly.

After a loss or poor performance

How would your athlete describe your mood?

After a win or strong performance

How would your athlete describe your mood?

What does the gap between those two responses tell your athlete about what you value?
You can be disappointed for them without being disappointed in them. What does that distinction look like for you in practice?
If your financial investment in their sport makes outcomes feel important to justify that cost — how do you separate that pressure from your athlete's development experience?
4

This Week's Practice — Three Specific Commitments

Your athlete learns responsibility by watching what you focus on. These commitments are specific, repeatable behaviors — not intentions.

After every game or practice this week:

Use only controllables questions. Notice your impulse to ask about results — and redirect.

Before the drive home, remind yourself: "My first question is about effort, not outcome."
If they bring up the result first, acknowledge it briefly, then redirect: "Tell me about your focus today."
Notice if your visible reaction after the game matches your stated values before the conversation even starts.

One teaching moment this week:

Find a natural moment — car ride, dinner, pre-practice — to draw or talk through the control circle together.

Ask: "What's something stressing you out about [upcoming game/practice]?" Then: "Is that inside or outside your circle?"
Follow up with: "What can you actually do about it?" — and let them answer. Don't fill the silence.
Let natural consequences teach preparation. If they skipped a skill-work session and it shows — don't rescue with excuses. Let the connection land.

My specific commitment this week:

Write one concrete behavior change you will make — not an intention, a specific action.

"Responsibility without pressure builds confidence.
Pressure without ownership builds anxiety."
