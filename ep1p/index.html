What's Your Post-Game Script?

Parent Self-Assessment  ·  5 Questions  ·  ~4 minutes

Most parents want to say the right thing after a tough game — but in the moment, we default to patterns we've never examined.

This assessment identifies your default response style and gives you three specific replacement phrases to practice this week.

Question 1 of 5

Your child walks off the field visibly upset after a hard loss. Your first instinct is to say:

"The score was 4–1. Your child is quiet, head down, near tears."

Question 2 of 5

Your child says "I'm so nervous" right before a big game. You respond:

"They're fidgeting and seem to be looking to you for something."

Question 3 of 5

The car ride home after a rough practice is silent. After a few minutes, you:

"Your child is staring out the window. They haven't said anything."

Question 4 of 5

Your child says: "I'm so bad at this. I hate this sport." You say:

"They just made a visible mistake in front of their teammates."

Question 5 of 5

After a strong performance, your child seems flat — not happy, not excited. You:

"They played well by every external measure. But they're quiet."

Why this matters scientifically: Research by Alison Wood Brooks (Harvard, 2014) confirms that reappraising anxiety as excitement — rather than telling someone to "calm down" — leverages the near-identical physiology of the two emotions. When you give your child language for their feelings, you're not doing soft parenting: you're doing applied neuroscience. Precise emotional labeling reduces amygdala activation (Lieberman et al., 2007).
