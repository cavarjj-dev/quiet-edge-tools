How to use this tool Research shows children learn self-talk patterns primarily through observation — not instruction. The most powerful self-talk lesson your athlete will ever receive is watching how you respond when you make a mistake. This tool has three parts: an audit of your own self-talk patterns, a framework reference card, and a pre-competition script builder. Fill it out honestly — this is for your eyes only.

The 4-Step Framework — When You Overhear Harsh Self-Talk

Catch & Redirect Framework — Parent Reference Card

Step 1
Catch & Redirect
Gently interrupt without dismissing. "Hey — let's pause on that." Then reframe identity from behavior: "You made a mistake. That's not the same as being stupid."
Step 2
Teach the Replacement
Offer the instructional script: "I'm still learning this. What can I do differently?" Give them specific language — not just "be positive."
Step 3
Teammate Standard
"Would you say that to your best teammate?" Activates empathy and helps them step outside the inner critic to examine it objectively.
Step 4
Pre-Script Pressure Moments
Before competition, plant instructional phrases so the brain has a default ready when pressure hits — no need to think, just execute.
3

Pre-Competition Script Builder — What You'll Say Before Games

These aren't pep talks. They are brief, calm, process-focused phrases you plant in the car, at warmup, or at the locker room goodbye — so your athlete has an instructional script ready before the pressure hits. The calmer and more matter-of-fact your delivery, the more their nervous system registers: "This is normal. I've been here before."

Phrase 1 — Reset Cue

For: when a mistake happens mid-competition

Example: "When something goes wrong today, just say 'next play' — and get back to your position."

Phrase 2 — Mistake Reframe

For: when they make an error and start the internal spiral

Example: "If you make an error, your job is to learn from it — not punish yourself. Say: 'What's next?'"

Phrase 3 — Pre-Start Anchor

For: right before competition begins — a calm, focused intention

Example: "Your job today is to play your game. Stay process-focused. Trust your training."

4

Reflection — After the Game or Practice

What self-talk pattern do you most consistently model — negative, hollow-positive, or instructional? What evidence supports that?
When your athlete uses harsh self-talk, what is your first impulse? Does that response help or hinder the Catch & Redirect process?
If your athlete's self-talk one year from now is shaped by one habit you build starting this week — what would that habit be?
