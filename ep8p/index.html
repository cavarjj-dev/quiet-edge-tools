Am I Effort-First?
One-Week Parent Reaction Tracker

A self-reflection tool for parents of youth athletes
Children don't learn from what we say — they learn from what we feel. If your mood after the game depends on the score, your athlete learns that results determine your approval. Research on parent attributional feedback (Weiner, 1985) shows that how parents emotionally respond to competition — not just what they say — directly shapes whether children develop resilience or anxiety over time. This tracker helps you audit what you're actually teaching.
"Be honest. Does your mood after competition depend more on the result — or their effort?"

Outcome-Oriented

Your reactions this week were primarily driven by results. That's common — most parents experience this because outcome-based evaluation is the default in competitive culture. The important insight is this: your child is almost certainly learning that results determine your approval — and by extension, their worth. That's not the lesson you intend to teach. Start with one change: this week, ask one effort-first question before any evaluation. Small, consistent shifts compound over time.

Mixed — Building Awareness

You're doing some things well and other habits still pull toward results. This is exactly the right stage — you have enough awareness to see the pattern, and you have effort-focused behaviors you can already build on. Focus on the specific result-focused behaviors you checked and create a concrete alternative for each one. The goal isn't perfection; it's calibration.

Effort-First

Your pattern this week reflects consistent effort-first parenting. Your athlete is receiving a clear, consistent signal: what you care about is within their control. That's the foundation of durable intrinsic motivation. Keep going — and consider sharing the specific questions and language that work for you with other parents in your community.

Section 3

Language Reframes: Result-Anchored → Effort-First

Some common phrases feel supportive but teach result-dependency. Here's what each one signals — and what to say instead.

What you might say What it actually teaches Effort-first alternative
"You tried your best." Consolation framing — implies the result was a failure requiring comfort "Tell me about a moment from today you're proud of."
"It's okay, you'll get 'em next time." Outcome-anchored — the implicit message is "this time was a loss" "How did you feel about your effort today?"
"You were the best one out there." Worth through comparison — depends on external ranking "I saw you pushing hard on that play. That's who you are."
"The coach should have played you more." Blame displacement — removes agency, teaches result-victimhood "What felt good about how you competed when you were in?"
"Don't cry, it was just a game." Emotion suppression — dismisses the feeling, teaches emotional hiding "That's disappointing. What part bothers you most?"
Section 4

Co-Parenting Scenarios

When parents send different signals, athletes learn to manage the emotional gap between them — not develop their own intrinsic standard. Here are scripts for three common tension points.

Scenario 1 — Result-focused comment on the drive home
"Your co-parent says 'You really should have scored on that chance' before the car reaches the parking lot exit."
Your redirect (to your athlete):
"I noticed you working really hard to create those chances. That's what I saw. How did that feel?"
Scenario 2 — Visible frustration/silence after a loss
"Your co-parent is quiet and visibly tense on the ride home. Your athlete is watching both of you."
Later, privately:
"I know that was frustrating to watch. I felt it too. But [Name] is reading our energy right now and learning what the result meant to us. Can we agree to lead with effort before anything else tonight?"
Scenario 3 — Ongoing effort vs. outcome gap
"Your co-parent consistently ties reactions and praise to results. Your athlete gets mixed signals depending on who's at the game."
A conversation to have before the next game:
"I want us to be consistent for [Name]'s sake — not because one of us is wrong, but because consistency is what actually builds their confidence. Can we try this: for the next two weeks, whoever picks them up asks one effort question before anything about the score? Something like 'What felt good today?' — just to see what happens."
Section 5

This Week's Commitment

Based on your log and audit, complete these two statements. Write specifically — not "I'll be more effort-focused" but what you will actually say and do.

Why this works: Effort attribution research (Weiner, 1985) shows that children who receive consistent effort-focused feedback from parents develop internal standards for evaluation — they stop needing the scoreboard to tell them how to feel. Autonomy-supportive parenting (Mageau & Vallerand, 2003) produces athletes with measurably lower anxiety and higher persistence. The post-competition conversation is the highest-leverage parenting moment in sport. What you say in the first five minutes sets the emotional frame for the entire week.
