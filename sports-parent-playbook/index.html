Module 1
Before the Game
The 2 hours before competition. How you show up — physically, verbally, and emotionally — directly transmits to your athlete's nervous system.
1
Stage 3 Interactive Assessment
Pre-Competition Environment Audit
Score your current pre-competition behaviors across 4 support behaviors and 4 routine disruptors to establish your environment profile. Identifies your single highest-impact disruptor.
Use it when: You want to understand what you're actually doing in the hours before competition — and what to stop.
📚 Cotterill (2010) — pre-performance routines reduce cognitive anxiety. Feldman (2007) — parent emotional arousal transmits to athlete through non-verbal signals.
→ Open Tool
2
Stage 3 Interactive Assessment
Game Day Protocol Card
Build a personalized game day behavior card — what to do, say, and avoid in the Before / During / After windows. Printable and pocket-sized.
Use it when: You want to stop winging it on game days and have a concrete behavior plan that keeps you in the support role.
📚 Beilock & Carr (2001) — verbal coaching during execution disrupts motor programs. Mageau & Vallerand (2003) — autonomy-supportive spacing reduces competitive anxiety.
→ Open Tool
3
Stage 3 Interactive Assessment
Parent Visualization Support Checklist
Score yourself across all 3 parent roles — Validate (recognize mental practice as real training), Protect (guard the quiet space), and Create Environment (build a reliable pre-competition window). Includes a guide for distinguishing productive visualization from worry spiraling.
Use it when: Your athlete uses visualization before competition and you're not sure if your presence, timing, or words are helping or getting in the way.
📚 Jeannerod (1994) — motor imagery activates the same neural pathways as physical execution. Holmes & Collins (2001) — PETTLEP model: quality of imagery conditions determines neural encoding strength. Feldman (2007) — parent emotional state transfers to athlete through non-verbal co-regulation.

Module 2
At the Sideline
Your presence during competition is a performance variable. These tools help you identify your sideline patterns and replace them with behaviors that actually help.
4
Stage 3 Interactive Assessment
Sideline Self-Check
A 5-dimension sideline behavior profile — scores your instruction, emotional signaling, body language, reaction to mistakes, and post-play communication. Outputs a personalized sideline challenge.
Use it when: You're not sure if your sideline presence is helping or hurting — or you've noticed your athlete's performance drops when you're watching.
📚 Beilock & Carr (2001) — skill-focused coaching under pressure increases conscious attention, degrades automaticity. Mageau & Vallerand (2003) — autonomy-supportive parenting reduces athlete anxiety.
→ Open Tool
5
Stage 2 Interactive Assessment
Sideline Reset Audit
8 sideline mistake scenarios — identify your default response pattern (Weight-Adder, Dismisser, or Reset-Supporter) and build a 7-day sideline practice plan with specific language shifts.
Use it when: Your athlete makes a mistake during a game and you want to understand and change how you respond in that moment.
📚 Gottman & DeClaire (1997) — parent emotional response to child mistakes directly shapes emotional regulation skill and resilience.

Module 3
After the Game
The 20-minute window after competition is the most important interaction you have as a sports parent. What you say — and don't say — shapes your athlete's relationship with competition itself.
6
Stage 2 Interactive Assessment
What's Your Post-Game Script?
Reveals your default response pattern — Instinctive Protector, Performance Manager, or Emotional Coach — and provides replacement scripts for each pattern. Most parents are surprised by their result.
Use it when: You want to understand your default post-game communication style and what it's actually doing to your athlete's emotional state.
📚 Brooks (2014) — anxiety reappraisal: how a parent frames their athlete's nervousness determines whether it helps or hinders performance. Lieberman et al. (2007) — affect labeling reduces amygdala activation.
→ Open Tool
7
Stage 2 Interactive Assessment
What's Your Failure Response Pattern?
Identifies your dominant failure response (Comfort Seeker, Amplifier, or Resilience Coach) and provides a 24-hour Acknowledge → Space → Process → Model protocol for the next setback.
Use it when: Your athlete has a bad game, a loss, or a significant mistake and you're not sure how to handle the next 24 hours.
📚 Gottman & DeClaire (1997) — emotion-coached children recover faster from setbacks. Dweck (2006) — parent attributions after failure shape child identity and motivation.
→ Open Tool
8
Stage 2 Interactive Assessment
Post-Game Question Guide
Identifies your post-game communication pattern (Teller, Reassurer, Asker, or Avoider) and teaches the Five Questions Framework that builds athlete self-evaluation rather than dependency on your feedback.
Use it when: You notice your athlete waits for your opinion instead of forming their own — or you want to build their internal performance standards.
📚 Deci & Ryan (2000) — asking vs. telling builds intrinsic motivation and accurate self-evaluation. Zimmerman (2000) — self-evaluation accuracy predicts greater resilience.
→ Open Tool
9
Stage 3 Interactive Assessment
Post-Game Conversation Self-Scorer
Rate your last 3 post-game conversations across 5 outcome-vs-process dimensions to see where your focus actually lands — including implicit pressure signals like silence, tone, and emotional reactions.
Use it when: You think your post-game conversations are supportive but your athlete seems stressed or guarded after games.
📚 Nicholls (1989) — ego-involving evaluation produces anxiety and fragile confidence. Mageau & Vallerand (2003) — outcome-contingent approval communicates conditional regard.
→ Open Tool

Module 4
At Home — Everyday Moments
The daily conversations, habits, and language patterns that shape your athlete's mental framework over a whole season — not just on game days.
10
Stage 3 Interactive Assessment
Training Load Audit
Calculates your athlete's weekly training load and flags overtraining warning signs across 5 categories. Outputs an Appropriate / Borderline / Overloaded rating with specific action steps.
Use it when: Your athlete seems tired, unmotivated, or injury-prone — or you're wondering if "more is more" in their training schedule.
📚 Ericsson et al. (1993) — volume alone does not predict expertise. Deci & Ryan (2000) — overtraining undermines intrinsic motivation and autonomy.
→ Open Tool
11
Stage 2 Interactive Assessment
The Voice in Their Head — Parent Self-Talk Audit
Audits your sideline self-talk patterns and how they're visible to your athlete. Children learn emotional regulation by observing parent responses under stress. Creates replacement scripts for high-pressure moments.
Use it when: You've noticed your athlete picks up on your anxiety, frustration, or disappointment — even when you haven't said anything.
📚 Bandura (1977) — social learning theory: children learn emotional regulation by observing parent responses under stress. Emotional contagion research confirms parent sideline affect influences athlete anxiety.
→ Open Tool
12
Stage 2 Interactive Assessment
Ownership Builds Confidence Toolkit
Identifies where your involvement crosses from support into ownership — and provides a responsibility scaffolding plan that builds athlete independence over time using autonomy-supportive language.
Use it when: Your athlete relies on you to handle logistics, decisions, or problems they could handle themselves — or you want to build their independence deliberately.
📚 Deci & Ryan (2000) — autonomy support predicts intrinsic motivation. Bandura (1997) — overprotective parenting undermines self-efficacy development.
→ Open Tool
13
Stage 2 Interactive Assessment
Progress Recognition Toolkit
Distinguishes between trait praise ("you're so talented"), outcome praise ("great game"), and progress recognition ("you held your mechanics under pressure today"). Creates personalized evidence-based recognition scripts.
Use it when: Your athlete seems to need your praise to feel good about their performance — or you want to build more durable confidence than wins and losses can provide.
📚 Dweck (2006) — process praise builds more durable confidence. Mueller & Dweck (1998) — specific process feedback increases persistence after failure.
→ Open Tool
14
Stage 2 Interactive Assessment
GPS Goal-Builder — Parent Edition
Audits your goal-related language for outcome vs. process orientation and teaches you to facilitate weekly process check-in conversations that build athlete autonomy rather than pressure.
Use it when: Conversations about goals feel tense, or your athlete seems to experience goals as pressure rather than direction.
📚 Locke & Latham (2002) — process goals reduce performance anxiety. Mageau & Vallerand (2003) — collaborative goal conversations build intrinsic motivation.
→ Open Tool
15
Stage 2 Interactive Assessment
Am I Effort-First? One-Week Reaction Tracker
A 7-day behavioral tracking log that captures your emotional reactions across game and practice events — and audits your post-competition behaviors for effort vs. result orientation.
Use it when: You want to know whether your actual behavior — across a full week, not just your intentions — is effort-focused or result-focused.
📚 Weiner (1985) — parent effort attributions build child resilience. Mageau & Vallerand (2003) — effort-based feedback in the post-competition window directly reduces athlete anxiety.
→ Open Tool

Bonus Module
For Two-Parent Families
When caregivers respond differently to the same situation, the inconsistency itself becomes a stressor for the athlete. These tools align your approach without requiring identical personalities.
16
Stage 2 Workbook
Parent Alignment Discussion Guide
A structured workbook for two caregivers to align on emotional regulation language, pre/during/post competition approaches, and a shared protocol — without requiring identical personalities.
Use it when: You and your co-parent respond differently after games and want a framework for getting on the same page.
📚 Gottman & DeClaire (1997) — consistent parenting environments reduce child anxiety. Mageau & Vallerand (2003) — autonomy support from both caregivers amplifies intrinsic motivation outcomes.
→ Open Guide
17
Stage 2 Interactive Assessment
Co-Parenting Through Youth Sports
Identifies co-parenting patterns that create emotional inconsistency and provides a unified emotional support protocol — a shared emotional language both caregivers can apply regardless of which one is present.
Use it when: Your athlete seems to behave or perform differently depending on which parent is at the game.
📚 Deci & Ryan (2000) — children need consistent, predictable emotional environments from caregivers to develop secure self-regulation.

18
Stage 2 Interactive Assessment
Multi-Child Family Navigator
Identifies the emotional regulation profile of each child in your household and provides a 4-Step Regulation Support Model (Name → Validate → Teach → Identify) with personalized scripts for each child's emotional style.
Use it when: You have more than one athlete-child and each needs different emotional support approaches — without you having to be a different person for each.
📚 Lieberman et al. (2007) — naming emotions is applied neuroscience. Gottman & DeClaire (1997) — emotion-coaching parents raise children who recover faster from setbacks.
