What Does Your Pre-Game
Language Signal?

A parent self-assessment for pressure moments — Ep. 1: Why Pressure Moments Are Developmental

This tool is about you — not your athlete.

Research on stress mindset (Crum et al., 2013) shows that parents transmit their interpretation of the pressure response through language — often without knowing it. When you say “just breathe,” you’re not just offering comfort. You’re telling your athlete that their nerves are a problem to solve.

This assessment helps you identify your default pattern, see what you’re actually signaling, and practice the language that lets the signal work for your athlete instead of against them.

Part 2 — Your Language Profile

Your Primary Pattern
The Neutralizer

Your instinct is to take the signal away. When your athlete shows nerves, you reach for calm — “just breathe,” “you’ll be fine,” “stop overthinking.” This impulse comes from love, and it’s the most common parent pattern in sports.

But Crum’s stress mindset research is clear: every time you frame the pressure response as a problem to fix, you teach your athlete that their body’s pre-competition signal means something is wrong. Over time, they learn to fight their own nervous system instead of use it.

Your athlete doesn’t need calm. They need to believe the signal is on their side.

Your Practice Focus Notice when you reach for a calming phrase. Before you say it, ask: “Is this for them, or is this for me?” You don’t need to have a better phrase ready yet — just noticing is the first step.
Your Primary Pattern
The Cheerleader

You replace the signal with confidence. Pep talks, reminders of hard work, proof of preparation — your instinct is to build your athlete up before competition. This comes from genuine belief in them, and it’s often what athletes say they love about their parents.

The subtle cost: relentless encouragement before a high-stakes moment can create pressure to feel confident, which competes with the natural pre-competition activation the body is producing. Athletes sometimes perform worse after high-energy pep talks because they’re managing your expectations alongside their own.

Try letting the signal stand. Trust that they already believe in themselves — your job isn’t to manufacture that belief in the final 15 minutes.

Your Practice Focus Before your next pep talk, pause. Ask: “Is this what they need, or what I need to give?” One grounding statement instead of three encouragements. Then stop.
Your Primary Pattern
The Observer

You already know that less is more in the pre-game window. You hold back, give space, and resist the urge to fix or coach. This restraint is a genuine strength — most parents never get here.

The next step isn’t doing less. It’s moving from strategic silence to intentional validation. There’s a meaningful difference between saying nothing and saying one specific thing that names the signal as useful. Right now your athlete may read your quiet as uncertainty. One well-placed sentence changes that.

You don’t need to speak more. You need to speak once — with intention.

Your Practice Focus Before the next competition, use the Stop → Notice → Validate exchange once: “I see it. That’s your body taking this seriously.” Then return to quiet. See what changes.
Your Primary Pattern
The Signal Reader

You’re already working with the research. You understand that the pre-competition nervous system signal is preparation — not panic — and your language reflects that. You name it, you don’t fight it, and you give your athlete permission to carry it into competition without treating it as a problem.

The challenge now is staying consistent under your own pressure: the important game, the visible tension on their face before a tryout, the moment when your anxiety rises and the old impulse comes back. It’s easier to validate in low-stakes moments.

Your Practice Focus Identify your own pressure threshold — which games or moments make it hardest to hold the Signal Reader pattern? Notice those moments. That’s where the work is.

Part 3 — Language Swap Bank

Common phrases, what they actually signal, and what to say instead. These aren’t scripts — they’re starting points. Find the version that sounds like you.

Scenario: Your athlete looks nervous before the game starts
What you might say
“Just breathe. You’ve got this. You’ll be fine.”
What it signals: “Your nerves are a problem. Let me fix them for you.”
Try instead
“I see it. That’s your body taking this seriously.”
What it signals: “The signal is valid. It’s working for you.”
Scenario: Your athlete is shaking or visibly tense during warmup
What you might say
“Relax — shake it out. Loosen up. You look so tight.”
What it signals: “Being this activated is the wrong state for competition.”
Try instead
[Say nothing. Optional: a hand on the shoulder, a nod.] “Let it work.”
What it signals: “I trust your body. You don’t need to perform calm for me.”
Scenario: Your athlete says “What if I mess up in front of everyone?”
What you might say
“That’s not going to happen! Stop thinking like that. You’re so prepared.”
What it signals: “That fear is irrational — override it.”
Try instead
“That’s a real question. What’s one thing you can control out there?”
What it signals: “The fear is valid. Let’s redirect it into something useful.”
Scenario: You give a pep talk before a big game
What you might say
“You’ve worked so hard for this. You’re ready. This is your moment. Let’s go!”
What it signals: “The weight of your preparation is on this moment. I need you to feel ready.”
Try instead
“Trust what you’ve built. Go play your game.”
What it signals: “I believe in your preparation. No pressure from me.”
Scenario: Car ride home after a tough loss
What you might say
“You just need to believe in yourself more” or an immediate positive spin.
What it signals: “Let’s get past this feeling fast. Fix your mindset.”
Try instead
“That was hard. I’m here. Talk whenever you’re ready.”
What it signals: “The feeling is allowed. You don’t have to process this on my timeline.”
