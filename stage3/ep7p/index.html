Parent Visualization Support Checklist

How consistently are you Validating, Protecting, and Creating the Environment for your athlete's mental practice?
This isn't a report card. It's a starting point. Research shows that visualization activates the same neural pathways as physical practice — but only when the conditions are right. As a parent, you are the guardian of those conditions. This checklist helps you see clearly where you're already supporting that, and where one small shift could make a real difference.

How to use it: Answer each item honestly, based on your actual recent behavior — not what you'd like to do. Rate how often each statement is true for you. Takes about 5 minutes.
✓
Part 1: Validate — I treat mental practice as real training
Does your athlete see that you take their mental preparation seriously?
When my athlete sits quietly before a competition, my first instinct is to leave them alone rather than fill the silence.
I avoid casual comments that frame visualization as unproductive (e.g., "stop spacing out," "you look nervous," "what are you thinking about?").
I have said something — even once — that acknowledged mental practice as valuable (e.g., "good idea to take a moment and picture it").
I believe (and could explain to my athlete) that visualization is backed by neuroscience — not just a "mental trick."

Part 2: Protect — I guard quiet, uninterrupted time
Mental practice needs undisturbed space. Are you the one creating it — or the one filling it?
There is a consistent 10-minute window in our pre-competition routine that is quiet and low-demand for my athlete.
I keep the car ride quiet (or match my athlete's energy level) rather than filling it with game-day talk, logistics, or encouragement they didn't ask for.
I avoid scheduling last-minute logistics, conversations, or demands in the final hour before my athlete competes.
When my athlete is in a pre-competition mental routine (eyes closed, listening to music, quiet warm-up), I resist the urge to interrupt — even with good intentions.

Part 3: Create Environment — I ask, then step back
The environment you create is the one your athlete prepares in. Are you asking what they need — and then honoring the answer?
I ask my athlete what they need before a competition, rather than assuming I know.
When my athlete says they need space or quiet, I give it — without taking it personally or checking in repeatedly.
My own emotional state before competitions is calm and stable — I'm not adding anxiety or urgency to the environment through my tone, pacing, or body language.
I stay out of my athlete's mental preparation process — I create the space, but I don't try to direct, guide, or teach the visualization itself.

Part 4: Recognize — I can tell visualization from worry
Both can look like a quiet athlete. Knowing the difference lets you intervene only when it actually helps.
I can recognize signs that my athlete's quiet moment is productive visualization (still body, slow breathing, calm expression) rather than anxious worry.
When I suspect my athlete is worrying (not visualizing), I know how to gently re-anchor them without over-correcting, solving, or dismissing their feelings.
I understand the difference between visualization (controlled, rehearsal-focused mental imagery) and worry (unfocused, catastrophizing, repetitive negative scenarios).
I resist the urge to "do something" every time my athlete seems anxious — I can sit with their pre-competition nerves without adding energy or pressure to it.

Your One Next Step
Why this matters: Research shows that vivid, first-person motor imagery activates the same neural pathways as physical practice (Jeannerod, 1994). The quality of those pathways depends on the conditions during mental rehearsal (Holmes & Collins, 2001). A parent's emotional state also transmits to the athlete through non-verbal signals — even without a word spoken (Feldman, 2007). You are the conditions.
