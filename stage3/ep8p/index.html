Step 4 of 5 — Your Language Reframe

Replace reactive language with curiosity

Below are reframes specific to the traps you checked. These aren't softer versions of the same message — they're a fundamentally different lens that treats fatigue as information, not failure.

The underlying shift: Reactive language asks "What's wrong with you?" Fatigue-aware language asks "What's happening in your body — and what does it tell us about training?" One adds shame to exhaustion. The other turns the observation into useful data.
Mageau & Vallerand (2003): Autonomy-supportive adults who minimize controlling language and acknowledge feelings produce athletes with higher intrinsic motivation and lower anxiety. Nicholls (1989): Framing late-game mistakes as character failure shifts athletes toward ego-involvement at the moment they are most depleted.
