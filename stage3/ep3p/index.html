Sideline Self-Check

Discover your sideline behavior pattern and get a personalized challenge for your next game — based on cognitive load research.

Before you begin

Bring a specific, recent game to mind — one where you were present on the sideline. Answer based on what you actually did, not what you intended. This is a starting point, not a report card. There are no wrong answers.

Why this matters: Research by Beilock & Carr (2001) shows that well-learned motor skills execute best automatically — when athletes "just play." Sideline instruction forces conscious re-engagement with movements the athlete has already trained, causing hesitation and degraded performance. Separately, Smoll & Smith (2002) found that a parent's nonverbal reactions (not just words) are the primary predictor of competitive anxiety in youth athletes. Your presence is always communicating — the question is what.
