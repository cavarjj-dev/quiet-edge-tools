Practice Pressure Support Audit
Are You Reinforcing or Rescuing?

8 questions · ~4 minutes · No sign-in required

Competition-like practice is designed to be uncomfortable. Your response to that discomfort — what you say in the car, how you frame a hard practice — either reinforces the training or quietly undoes it. This audit helps you identify where your instincts are aligned and where they might be working against your athlete's development.

Part 1 of 1
How do you typically respond?

Rate each statement honestly. Think about how you actually respond — not how you’d like to.

1When my athlete comes home from a stressful, competitive practice, I resist the urge to contact the coach or suggest the session was too hard.
2After a hard practice, my first question to my athlete is about how they handled it — not about whether the session was fair or necessary.
3I can explain to my athlete — without negativity toward the coach — why competition-like practice is designed to feel uncomfortable.
4When my athlete says “I don’t feel ready” before a competition, I validate the feeling without trying to eliminate it or rush past it.
5I allow my athlete to sit with post-practice frustration or exhaustion without immediately stepping in with advice, solutions, or reassurance.
6I avoid comparing how my athlete handles practice pressure to their teammates or other kids.
7When my athlete performs better in practice than in games, I see it as a gap to bridge — not as a coaching problem or a sign that something is wrong.
8After a particularly demanding practice, I use language that frames the challenge as preparation — not as something my athlete has to endure or get through.
